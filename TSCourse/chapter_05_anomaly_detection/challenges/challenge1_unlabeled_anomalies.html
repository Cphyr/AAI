Challenge 01 - Flag Anomalous Tracks in Unlabeled Data#

20,000 tracks. No labels. Somewhere between 0.5 % and 2 % of them are anomalous — you do not know which, and you do not know the rate.

The deliverable is not a detector. It is a defended evaluation protocol. Specifically: given a budget of 500 analyst labels, produce an estimate of precision and recall at your chosen operating threshold, with a confidence interval, and defend every choice in it.

Honest baselines (Chapter 0 L02) — all three are cheap, so there is no excuse:

  1. Isolation forest on ~15 kinematic features.

  2. kNN distance in the same feature space.

  3. Negative log-likelihood under a constant-velocity Kalman filter fitted on the whole corpus (Chapter 2 L01).

Evaluation protocol (Chapter 1 L03): tracks carry a timestamp. Fit the normal-behaviour model on the first 70 % of days and score the last 30 %. No random splits — drift is part of the problem.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

RNG = np.random.default_rng(99)
plt.rcParams.update({"figure.figsize": (11, 3.2), "axes.grid": True, "grid.alpha": 0.3})

N_TRACKS = 20000
TRUE_RATE = RNG.uniform(0.005, 0.02)          # you are not told this


def make_corpus(n=N_TRACKS, rate=TRUE_RATE, seed=0):
    """Tracks as (day, features-generating params); returns raw position sequences."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    day = rng.integers(0, 100, n)
    is_anom = rng.random(n) < rate
    kind = np.where(is_anom, rng.choice(["loiter", "dash", "erratic"], n), "normal")
    out = []
    for i in range(n):
        T = int(rng.integers(120, 240))
        v = rng.normal(200, 20)
        w = rng.normal(0, 0.002, T)
        if kind[i] == "loiter":
            w = 0.04 * np.sign(np.sin(2 * np.pi * np.arange(T) / rng.integers(40, 70)))
            v *= 0.6
        elif kind[i] == "dash":
            v *= 1.9
        elif kind[i] == "erratic":
            w = rng.normal(0, 0.02, T)
        # slow drift over the corpus: later days are sampled more sparsely (a real-world nuisance)
        drop = 0.05 + 0.25 * day[i] / 100
        hdg = np.cumsum(w) + rng.uniform(0, 2 * np.pi)
        speed = v * (1 + 0.05 * rng.normal(0, 1, T))
        pos = np.cumsum(np.c_[speed * np.cos(hdg), speed * np.sin(hdg)], axis=0)
        pos += rng.normal(0, 30, pos.shape)
        keep = np.sort(rng.choice(T, max(20, int(T * (1 - drop))), replace=False))
        out.append((day[i], np.arange(T)[keep].astype(float), pos[keep]))
    return out, is_anom, kind


corpus, TRUTH, KIND = make_corpus()
print(f"{len(corpus)} tracks over {max(c[0] for c in corpus) + 1} days")
print("The truth vector exists ONLY so this challenge can be graded. Using it anywhere except in")
print("Part 5 (where you check your estimate against it) defeats the entire exercise.")

Part 1 - Which anomaly are you looking for?#

Chapter 5 L01 lists four types: point, contextual, collective, group. Say which one you are targeting before you build anything, because it determines the scoring unit (per time step? per track?) and the alert unit.

Part 2 - Build the score#

Feature extraction is Chapter 3 territory: use the smoothed state, not finite differences. Then pick your framing — density/likelihood, reconstruction, or discriminative-with-few-labels.

One trap this corpus is built to spring: the sampling rate degrades over time (later days drop more points). Any detector that sees gap statistics will happily learn “recent = anomalous”. Check your top alerts against day before you believe anything.

def features(track):
    """Minimal starter feature set. Extend it - and justify each addition."""
    _, t, p = track
    dt = np.maximum(np.diff(t), 1e-6)
    v = np.diff(p, axis=0) / dt[:, None]
    sp = np.linalg.norm(v, axis=1)
    hdg = np.unwrap(np.arctan2(v[:, 1], v[:, 0]))
    w = np.diff(hdg) / dt[1:]
    path = np.linalg.norm(np.diff(p, axis=0), axis=1).sum()
    return np.array([
        np.median(sp), sp.std(), np.percentile(sp, 95),
        np.median(np.abs(w)), np.percentile(np.abs(w), 95),
        np.mean(np.abs(w) > 0.02), np.sum(np.diff(np.sign(w)) != 0) / len(w),
        np.linalg.norm(p[-1] - p[0]) / max(path, 1e-9),
        len(t), t[-1] - t[0], len(t) / max(t[-1] - t[0], 1e-9),
    ])


F = np.array([features(c) for c in corpus])
days = np.array([c[0] for c in corpus])

Part 3 - Design the label budget (the core of this challenge)#

You have 500 labels. Chapter 5 L01 tells you why labelling the top 500 scores is the wrong move: it estimates precision at the top and gives you no information about recall.

Design a stratified sample with inverse-probability weighting:

  1. Partition the score range into strata. Justify the boundaries (why these, why this many?).

  2. Allocate the 500 labels across strata. High-score strata hold the positives, so sample them heavily — but no stratum may be sampled at zero, or recall becomes unestimable.

  3. Estimate, with Horvitz-Thompson weights \(1/\pi_h\): \(\widehat{TP}, \widehat{FP}, \widehat{FN}\) and hence precision and recall at your threshold.

  4. Put an interval on both. Bootstrap within strata is the least painful route.

The oracle below is your analyst: it costs one label per call and refuses after 500.

class LabelBudget:
    def __init__(self, truth, budget=500):
        self._truth, self.budget, self.used, self.seen = truth, budget, 0, {}

    def label(self, i):
        i = int(i)
        if i in self.seen:
            return self.seen[i]
        if self.used >= self.budget:
            raise RuntimeError(f"budget exhausted at {self.budget} labels - that is the point")
        self.used += 1
        self.seen[i] = bool(self._truth[i])
        return self.seen[i]

Part 4 - Choose an operating point#

The analyst team can process 50 alerts per day, and the stream carries ~200 tracks per day. Convert that into a threshold, and report the estimated precision and recall at that threshold with intervals. Do not report ROC-AUC as your headline number — explain in one sentence why it is misleading at this base rate.

Part 5 - Post-mortem (required)#

  1. Check your estimate. Only now, compute the true precision/recall using TRUTH. Did your interval cover the truth? If not, which assumption in your sampling design failed?

  2. The estimated base rate. Estimate the anomaly rate from your stratified sample and compare to TRUE_RATE. How wide is the interval with 500 labels — and is that width acceptable for a decision?

  3. What is at the top? Inspect your top 20 alerts. How many are the sampling artifact (day-correlated gap statistics) rather than behaviour? Fix it and re-run: how much does precision change?

  4. Rare vs. interesting. Of the three anomaly kinds (loiter, dash, erratic), which does your detector find, and which does it miss entirely? Explain the miss in terms of your normal-behaviour model’s assumptions.

  5. Drift. Score the first 30 days and the last 30 days separately. Does your threshold hold, or does the alert rate move? Propose a monitoring rule.

  6. The budget question. If you were given 5,000 labels instead of 500, would you spend them on a better estimate — or train a supervised classifier and evaluate that with the remaining labels? Answer with numbers, not intuition.
